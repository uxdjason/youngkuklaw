## Case Facts

Roger Street granted Wendy Mountford the right to occupy two rooms in his property under an agreement styled as a "licence." The agreement contained a declaration that it did not create a tenancy and reserved the right for Street to enter the rooms. Mountford had exclusive possession of the rooms. Mountford subsequently claimed that she had a tenancy, which would afford her statutory rent protection.

## Held

The House of Lords held that the agreement, despite its express labelling as a "licence," created a lease (tenancy). Where residential accommodation is granted for a term at a rent with exclusive possession, the landlord providing no services, the grant is a tenancy. The court must look at the substance of the agreement, not its form or label.

## Ratio Decidendi

1. The hallmarks of a tenancy are: exclusive possession, for a term, at a rent.
2. If these three requirements are satisfied, a tenancy is created unless there are special circumstances (such as a service occupancy or family arrangement).
3. The parties cannot alter the legal nature of their relationship simply by choosing a different label (the "cat cannot be called a dog" principle).

## Obiter Dicta

Lord Templeman noted that a licence to occupy land only prevents the occupier from being a trespasser, whereas a tenancy vests an estate in the land in the tenant, giving them the right to exclude the world, including the landlord, subject to any rights reserved by the lease.

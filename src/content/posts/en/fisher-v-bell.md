## Case Facts

The defendant, James Charles Bell, was a retail shopkeeper operating Bell's Music Shop at 15–16, The Arcade, Broadmead, Bristol. On 26 October 1959, Police Constable John Kingston observed a flick knife displayed in the shop window with a price ticket bearing the words "Ejector knife – 4s." When confronted by the officer, the defendant remarked, "Why do manufacturers still bring them round for us to sell?" — a comment that formed part of the factual record.

An information was preferred on 14 December 1959, alleging that the defendant had offered the knife for sale contrary to s 1(1) [Restriction of Offensive Weapons Act 1959](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/Eliz2/7-8/37). That provision makes it an offence to manufacture, sell, hire, offer for sale or hire, lend or give a flick knife. Notably, the Act contained no definition of "offer for sale".

The matter was heard by Bristol justices on 3 February 1960. The justices dismissed the information, holding that the window display constituted an invitation to treat rather than an offer for sale. The prosecutor appealed to the Divisional Court of the Queen's Bench Division, which heard the appeal on 10 November 1960.

## Held

The appeal was dismissed. The Divisional Court held that, in the absence of any statutory definition extending the meaning of "offer for sale" within the Restriction of Offensive Weapons Act 1959, that phrase must be given the meaning attributed to it under the ordinary law of contract.

Under ordinary contractual principles, the display of an article in a shop window, even with a price ticket attached, is merely an invitation to treat and not an offer for sale. An offer for sale, in the contractual sense, is a proposition the acceptance of which would constitute a binding contract. A shop display, by contrast, invites prospective customers to make an offer, which the shopkeeper may then accept or decline. Accordingly, the defendant had not "offered" the knife for sale within the meaning of the Act and had committed no offence.

The cases of Keating v Horwood (1926) and Wiles v Maddison [1943], which were advanced by the prosecutor in support of a broader construction, were considered and distinguished by the court.

## Ratio Decidendi

The ratio of this case is that the display of goods in a shop window with a price ticket is an invitation to treat, not an offer for sale, as those concepts are understood in the general law of contract. Where a criminal statute uses the phrase "offer for sale" without providing a statutory definition or any express extension of its ordinary meaning, the court must apply the contractual meaning of that term. Consequently, such a window display cannot constitute an offence of "offering for sale" under the Restriction of Offensive Weapons Act 1959.

This principle draws upon the foundational common law position established in Timothy v Simpson (1834), which is cited as the original authority for the proposition that displaying goods in a shop window amounts only to an invitation to treat. The same principle had been affirmed in [Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain v Boots Cash Chemists (Southern) Ltd [1953]](/pharmaceutical-society-v-boots), which was cited in argument before the court and remains a leading authority on the offer and invitation to treat distinction. The case is also closely connected to [Partridge v Crittenden [1968]](/partridge-v-crittenden), in which an advertisement in a periodical was similarly held to be an invitation to treat rather than an offer.

## Obiter Dicta

In the course of his judgement, the presiding judge observed that, at first sight, it seemed absurd that knives of the sort in question could not be manufactured, sold, hired, lent or given, yet could apparently be displayed freely in shop windows. He acknowledged this might well be a *casus omissus* — a gap inadvertently left by Parliament — but stated plainly that it was not for the court to supply the omission. This remark is a frequently cited illustration of the limits of judicial statutory interpretation: courts are bound to interpret the words Parliament has actually enacted and may not fill legislative gaps, however inconvenient or anomalous the result may appear. The appropriate remedy for such an omission lies with Parliament, not with the judiciary. The Restriction of Offensive Weapons Act 1959 was subsequently amended to address this lacuna.

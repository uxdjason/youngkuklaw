## Case Facts

The case concerned the placement of an advertisement in a commercial magazine reading "Bramblefinch cocks, Bramblefinch hens, 25s each." The claimant, acting on behalf of the RSPCA, alleged that the defendant had contravened the [Protection of Birds Act 1954](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/Eliz2/2-3/30/contents/enacted). Specifically, it was argued that the advertisement constituted an "offer for sale" of protected birds.

## Held

The Queen's Bench Division held that the advertisement amounted to an invitation to treat. Accordingly, the advertisement did not constitute an offer for sale, and the defendant was found not to have contravened the [Protection of Birds Act 1954](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/Eliz2/2-3/30/contents/enacted).

## Ratio Decidendi

1. A commercial advertisement is to be regarded as an invitation to treat and is not to be construed as a legally binding offer.

2. A contract is formed when a person responding to an advertisement expresses an intention to purchase and the seller accepts that expression.

3. The role of the court is to reach its determination within the bounds of established legal definitions and interpretation, without going beyond the intention of the legislature.

This case is recognised, alongside [Fisher v Bell [1961]](/fisher-v-bell) and [Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain v Boots Cash Chemists (Southern) Ltd [1953]](/pharmaceutical-society-v-boots), as a leading authority establishing the doctrine of invitation to treat.

## Essential Elements of a Legally Binding Contract

Under English law, three essential elements must be present for a contract to be formed with legal force: agreement, contractual intention, and consideration. Agreement refers to the mutual assent between the contracting parties; a valid agreement consists of an offer and an acceptance. Contractual intention concerns whether the parties intended to be bound by contract, whilst consideration addresses whether something of value or a counter-performance was provided in exchange for the contractual act.

## Offer

For a valid contract to be formed, there must be an agreement comprising an offer and an acceptance.

According to [Prof. Treitel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guenter_Treitel), an offer is an expression of willingness to contract on specified terms. Accordingly, an offer must clearly carry the intention that legal relations will arise immediately upon its acceptance by the other party.

In order to determine whether an offer has been validly made, English courts apply an objective test, assessing whether a reasonable person would regard the statement in question as constituting an offer. This is one of the important principles of English contract law established through case law.

In [Smith v Hughes (1871)](/smith-v-hughes-case), the court determined the existence of a contract by reference to objectively ascertainable statements rather than the subjective intention of the parties. In [Allied Marine Transport v Vale do Rio Doce Navegacao [1985]](/allied-marine-ltd-v-vale-do-rio-doce-sa), the objective test was applied to assess the clarity of contractual terms and the intention of the parties.

## Invitation to Treat

An invitation to treat is a statement that merely invites a potential contracting party into negotiations or indicates the possibility of a transaction; it does not in itself constitute an offer.

For example, goods displayed in the windows or on the shelves of a department store or shop are treated not as an offer but as an invitation to treat. Similarly, goods laid out for customers to select in self-service stores such as supermarkets are regarded as an invitation to treat rather than an offer.

In [Fisher v Bell [1961]](/fisher-v-bell), the court confirmed that the display of goods constitutes an invitation to treat — an invitation to discuss a purchase — rather than an offer. In [Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain v Boots Cash Chemists (Southern) Ltd [1953]](/pharmaceutical-society-v-boots), it was held that goods displayed in a self-service store amount to an invitation to treat, and that it is the customer's act of selecting goods and communicating an intention to pay that constitutes the offer.

Advertisements are also generally regarded as an invitation to treat rather than an offer. However, by way of exception, where an advertisement contains a clear intention to be bound contractually, it may be treated as an offer. Furthermore, where an advertisement offers a specific reward or prize, it is treated as an offer, and where a person performs the act stipulated in order to obtain that reward, such performance is recognised as an acceptance of the offer.

In [Partridge v Crittenden [1968]](/partridge-v-crittenden), the court confirmed that a general advertisement does not carry legal binding force. In [Carlill v Carbolic Smoke Ball Company [1893]](/carlill-v-carbolic-smoke-ball-company), it was held that where an advertisement contains a clear intention to be bound, it is to be treated as an offer. In [Williams v Carwardine (1833)](/williams-v-carwardine), it was held that a reward advertisement constitutes an offer and that performance of the act required to obtain the reward amounts to an acceptance.

## Bilateral and Unilateral Contracts

A bilateral contract arises where the contracting parties make promises to one another — that is, where one party makes a promise and the other party makes a promise in return. For example, Company A promises to supply 100 goods, and in response Company B promises to pay a specified sum. In this case, offer and acceptance occur simultaneously, and both Company A and Company B immediately assume contractual obligations.

A unilateral contract, however, operates differently. It arises where one party promises a reward in exchange for the performance of a specified act, and the other party then performs that act. The promise is communicated unilaterally rather than bilaterally. For example, where Company C announces — whether by advertisement or otherwise — that it will provide a certain reward upon the performance of a specified act, that announcement itself constitutes an offer. If Consumer D performs the stipulated act, that performance is treated as an acceptance of Company C's offer. Accordingly, Company C and Consumer D become parties to a contract at the moment Consumer D completes the act, and both parties immediately assume contractual obligations. The case of [Williams v Carwardine (1833)](/williams-v-carwardine), mentioned above, is an example of precisely this situation.

## Auction

In auction transactions, an auctioneer's request for bids constitutes an invitation to treat rather than an offer. Where a person participates in an auction and makes a bid in response to the auctioneer's request, that bid constitutes an offer. The auctioneer may accept the offer made in the form of a bid, or may wait for a higher offer.

The relevant statutory provision is [s 57(2) Sale of Goods Act 1979](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1979/54/section/57).

Since an auctioneer acts as the agent of the original owner of the goods being auctioned, a bilateral contract between the successful bidder and the original owner is formed immediately upon the auctioneer's acceptance of a particular bid. Accordingly, in a sale without reserve, the owner of the goods cannot refuse to be bound by the contract even if the accepted bidding price is unsatisfactory. If the owner refuses to sell to the successful bidder, this will constitute a breach of contract, rendering the owner liable in damages to that bidder.

In [Barry v Davies [2001]](/barry-v-davies), the court held that in an auction conducted without reserve, an auctioneer cannot refuse to contract with the highest bidder.

## Tender

Where a public body or company issues a request for tender to businesses and individuals for the purpose of carrying out various projects, this is generally regarded as an invitation to treat. Where businesses or individuals respond by submitting a tender, they are considered to have made an offer; and where a particular tender is accepted, the offer is accepted and a valid contract is formed.

However, in special circumstances where certain conditions are satisfied, a request for tender may itself constitute an offer, such that a unilateral contract is validly formed upon the submission of the tender.

In [Harvela Investments v Royal Trust Canada [1986]](/harvela-investments-ltd-v-royal-trust-of-canada), the court confirmed that a tender proposal may be treated as an offer and that a contract is formed upon acceptance. In [Blackpool and Fylde Aero Club Ltd v Blackpool Borough Council [1990]](/blackpool-and-fylde-aero-club-v-blackpool-borough-council), it was held that a request for tender satisfying certain conditions may give rise to a unilateral contract.

## Case Facts

The defendant, William Carwardine, was the brother of Walter Carwardine, whose body was discovered in the River Wye on 12 April 1831 bearing marks of violence. Walter had been murdered on the night of 24 March 1831. On 25 April 1831, the defendant caused a handbill to be published offering two separate rewards: £10 to anyone who would apprehend a woman named Sarah Coley and lodge her in gaol, and £20 to whoever would give such information as might lead to a discovery of the murder of Walter Carwardine, payable upon conviction. The handbill further promised that any person concerned in or privy to the murder — except the actual perpetrator — would receive a reward and every exertion to procure a pardon. Information was to be given and applications for the reward made to the defendant at Holmer, near Hereford, or to his solicitor, Mr Watkyns, in Hereford.

At the Hereford Summer Assizes of 1831, two men named Pugh and Connop were tried for Walter Carwardine's murder and acquitted. The plaintiff, Mary Anne Williams, had been examined as a witness against them at that trial. She therefore had knowledge of the handbill and its terms before she came to give the information that forms the basis of her claim.

Shortly after the acquittal of Pugh and Connop, the plaintiff was dreadfully beaten by a man named William Williams. Believing she was not likely to recover from her injuries, she made a voluntary disclosure to Mr Howells, the swordbearer of the city of Hereford. On 23 August 1831, she made a sworn deposition before a magistrate, William Milton, in which she stated expressly that she made it "to ease her conscience, and in hopes of forgiveness hereafter" — and not in consequence of the reward offered in the handbill. In her deposition, the plaintiff gave a detailed eyewitness account of seeing Joseph Pugh, William Williams, a man named Matthews, and Sarah Coley attacking Walter Carwardine. She recounted hearing the victim say "For God's sake do not murder me," hearing a dreadful blow, and hearing him fall and groan as if dying.

In consequence of the information given by the plaintiff, William Williams, Joseph Pugh, and John Matthews were convicted of the murder of Walter Carwardine at the Hereford Assizes on 20 March 1832. The plaintiff subsequently brought her claim against the defendant in Assumpsit, her declaration containing eight counts, the first five of which pleaded the handbill offer and her provision of information leading to conviction in various formulations.

The case was heard at the Hereford Assizes on 22 March 1833, before Mr Justice Parke sitting on nisi prius. Subsequent King's Bench proceedings arising from the case are reported at 4 B & Ad 621, at which Denman CJ, Littledale J, Parke J, and Patteson J were present.

The central question before the court was whether the plaintiff was entitled to the £20 reward in circumstances where the jury found that she had not given the information in consequence of the offered reward, but rather from other, entirely personal motives.

## Held

The court held that the plaintiff was entitled to the £20 reward. The jury's finding that her motive for providing the information was personal — to ease her conscience rather than to claim the reward — was held to be immaterial to the question of her entitlement. The plaintiff had knowledge of the handbill and had performed the condition it stipulated, namely providing information that led to the conviction of the murderers. That was sufficient to establish her right to the reward.

The court further held that the first person to give the requisite information is entitled to the reward, and that the motive of that person in giving the information is not a material consideration. It was additionally noted that if two persons go together to give the information, they must bring a joint action for the reward.

## Ratio Decidendi

The ratio decidendi of this case establishes a foundational principle concerning the acceptance of unilateral offers. A published advertisement or handbill offering a reward upon the fulfilment of specified conditions constitutes a binding offer capable of acceptance by any person who, with knowledge of the offer, performs the required act. Two conditions must be satisfied for a claimant to be entitled to the reward: first, the claimant must have knowledge of the offer at the time of performing the required act; and secondly, the claimant must actually perform the condition stipulated in the offer.

Critically, once those two requirements are met, the subjective motive of the person performing the condition is legally irrelevant. The law does not require that the offeree was induced by the offer itself; it is sufficient that the offeree knew of the offer and performed the required act. The formation of a binding unilateral contract depends on knowledge and performance, not on the psychological motivation behind the acceptance.

This case is an early authority on the nature of unilateral contracts — sometimes called "offers to the world" — and sits alongside the later and more frequently cited authority of [Carlill v Carbolic Smoke Ball Company [1893]](/carlill-v-carbolic-smoke-ball-company) as establishing the framework for how such offers operate. The principle that motive is irrelevant to acceptance was later the subject of further analysis in [R v Clarke (1927)](/r-v-clarke), where the High Court of Australia distinguished the present case on the basis that Clarke had entirely forgotten the existence of the offer when he gave the relevant information, thereby lacking knowledge of it — a distinction that serves to confirm that knowledge remains the essential, non-negotiable requirement.

## Obiter Dicta

The court's observation that if two persons jointly provide the qualifying information they must bring a joint action for the reward may be regarded as obiter, since the facts before the court involved a sole informant. This remark was not necessary for the determination of the plaintiff's individual claim but provides useful practical guidance on how competing or collaborative claims to a unilateral reward should be handled procedurally.
